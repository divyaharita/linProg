3.1"Less than" and "Greater than" : A Quick Review

We have been focusing on points that lie exactly on a given line. These are points that satisfy the corresponding linear equation (equality).

In linear programming, we often need to ask questions like this :

  • What do I need to do if I want my manufacturing cost to be less than Rs. 1000 per unit?
  • What do I need to do if I need to get more than 400 calories from eating apples and bananas, but I also want to make sure that I get less than 600 calories?

These are examples of inequalites based on linear equations. Obviously, if we move off the line, we are saying that the LHS of the equation is now different from the RHS. Points on the line satisfy the equality, but points lying anywhere in the area above / below the line will satisfy the inequalities.

Look at the graph and think for a moment. Which area contains points where we get more than 500 calories? Which area contains points where we get less than 500 calories?

Now move the "Minimum Cals" slider slowly from one end to the other and watch the movement of the line. You may notice one important property of the line's movement that really stands out very clearly.

What do you think this important property of the line's behaviour is?

  • The line moves parallel to itself, i.e. its slope remains constant.

3.2Meeting More Than One Condition (Multiple Constraints)

In the previous tab (3.1) we learned how to identify areas that satisfy a single constraint or condition, e.g. "calories must be greater than 500". It's quite simple -- we just consider the area above the line (for >) or below the line (for <).

But in real world situations, we often need to find an optimal solution that will satisfy multiple constraints. In our example of 500 calories, suppose we find that eating too many calories can be as bad as not getting enough calories. Let's say we need to make sure we don't eat more than 600 calories. Also, let's say we are OK if we eat at least 400 calories, but not less than 400.

These two "constraints" or "conditions" give us these two equations:

A * X + B * Y > 400    (the violet line in the graph)

A * X + B * Y < 600    (the green line)

Clearly, the shaded region between the two lines is the set of points where the number of calories is >400 as well as <600. Any choice of X and Y from within this region, will satisfy both of the above equations. This area is, in fact, bounded by the X axis, the violet line, the Y axis and the green line.

We say that this region is the "feasible region" for our problem, given the two constraints that we have here.

3.3A situation that has NO Solution!

Now move the "Minimum calories" slider to 750. What does this setting mean, in words? We are saying that we want to consume more than 750 AND less than 600 calories. Obviously, we are asking for an impossible result. This is shown by the red shading in the in-between area.

So in linear programming, we may run into problems that have no solution -- where there is no feasible region.

3.4All Good Things come at a COST !

Okay, so now we know that if we eat, say for example, 3 apples and 4 bananas, we will get about 510 calories. And this is well within the "feasible region", i.e. it satisfies our constraints (requirements/conditions) quite nicely. This point (3 apples, 4 bananas) is shown in the chart using a red star ( * ) marker.

That's all well and good, you say --- but apples and bananas cost money! And everyone wants to plan their spending in such a way that they live healthy while saving money at the same time. So it's time to bring the price factor into our problem at this point. All right, let's say apples cost   ₹30   each and bananas cost   ₹5  each.
